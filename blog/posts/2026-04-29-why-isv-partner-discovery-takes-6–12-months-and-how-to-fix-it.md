## *The definitive guide to the most underestimated problem in ISV go-to-market*

Ask any ISV founder what their biggest go-to-market challenge is, and you'll hear one of two answers: "finding customers" or "finding the right channel partners." The first has a well-developed solution ecosystem —CRMs, SDRs, demand gen agencies, and a dozen methodologies. The second barely has a name.

Partner discovery — the process of identifying, evaluating, and shortlisting channel partners worth approaching — is the most consistently underestimated problem inISV go-to-market. And in my 20 years of building and advising enterprise channel partnerships, I've watched it cost ISVs not just months of time, but entire market entry windows.

*"The average ISV  spends 6–12 months finding their first right-fit channel partner. That's not  a research finding. That's what I hear in almost every first conversation  with an ISV founder."*

## **Why does it take so long?**

The answer isn't laziness or poor prioritization. It's a structural problem — and understanding it is the first step to solving it.

### 1. The wrong tools for the job

The partner tech market has invested heavily in co-sell execution, marketplace automation, and partner relationship management. These are excellent tools —for managing partners you've already found. None of them help you answer the first question: which partners should you be approaching in the first place?

MostISVs fall back on the same discovery methods that have existed for two decades:conference networking, analyst relationship maps, LinkedIn searches, and ecosystem directories. All of these tell you who exists. None of them tell you who fits.

### 2. Fit is invisible until it isn't

The cruel thing about wrong-fit partner relationships is that they look identical to right-fit ones in the early stages. Both start with a warm introduction or a promising first call. Both produce LOI conversations. Both require months of investment before the fit problem surfaces.

Bythe time you discover that a partner's client base doesn't overlap with yours, or that they don't have the capacity to prioritize a new ISV product, or that they've never successfully activated a partnership at your price point — you've already spent the relationship capital that could have been invested in the right partner.

### 3. The qualification gap

MostISVs qualify prospects before approaching them for sales conversations. Persona fit, company size, tech stack, budget indicators — the qualification criteria are well defined. The same discipline almost never exists for partner outreach.ISVs approach partners based on name recognition, referrals, and gut feel, and discover fit problems the expensive way.

#### The five dimensions of partner fit

After two decades of doing this work, I've found that fit comes down to five dimensions — and a right-fit partner needs to score well on most of them beforea conversation is worth having.

→ **Ecosystem Fit** — do they operate in the same technology ecosystem as your product? A partner deeply embedded in a competing ecosystem is almost never a good bet.

→ **Client Overlap** — do their existing clients look like your ideal customers? Not your total addressable market — your actual best customers.

→ **Geographic Coverage** — are they active in the markets you're targeting in the next 12–18 months?

→ **Partnership History** — have they successfully activated ISV partnerships before? At a similar price point and complexity? Or do their past partnerships stall at LOI?

→ **Capacity and Motivation** — are they genuinely available? Or are they over-committed and adding partners they won't prioritize?

The problem is that answering these five questions for a list of 50 candidate partners — which is typically what a thorough ecosystem map produces — requires enormous manual effort. LinkedIn research, analyst reports, direct out reach, and often a network of calls with people who know the partner community. This is why 6–12 months is the norm, not the exception.

## How to compress the timeline

### Start with the brief, not the list

The most common mistake ISVs make is starting with "who are all the partners in my ecosystem?" rather than "what does a right-fit partner look like for my specific product, ICP, and market?" The brief — a precise definition of ideal partner characteristics — is the foundation everything else is built on. Without it, any list you generate is noise.

### Use signal analysis, not directory browsing

Directory browsing tells you who exists. Signal analysis tells you who fits. The difference between the two is the difference between a list and a shortlist.Before approaching any partner, you should be able to answer the five fit questions above — not from a conversation with the partner, but from intelligence gathered before you make contact.

### Qualify before you engage — not during

The discovery and qualification phases should be complete before the first outreach email is sent. This sounds obvious, but it's the opposite of how most ISVs operate. The first meeting with a potential partner should not be a qualification call. It should be a fit-confirmed conversation about how you work together.
